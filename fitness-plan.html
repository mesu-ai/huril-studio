Aerial Yoga

    What is it?

    Aerial yoga combines traditional asanas (yoga’s physical postures) and the philosophies of yoga with the art of the air. Silk fabrics and/or ropes were hung from above to help practitioners create shapes. You can be fully supported by the bristles – even lying down completely, such as in a hammock – or wrap the bristles around certain parts of your body, keeping other parts off the floor . It is believed that fully hanging or hanging individual body parts creates traction and opens your body more gently and intuitively than if you were on the floor. Silk and rope can also help with balance. While many aerial yoga classes have an acrobatic element, an increasing number of classes and teachers are using aerial silk in a much more therapeutic way. Jo Stewart, co-owner of Garden of Yoga, a Melbourne-based studio that offers a variety of aerial yoga classes, is a strong advocate of using aerial silk in a more therapeutic way. As she says, “the flexibility and support of the fabric give people access to new movements. It can also reduce the load on the wrists and knees, which can sometimes limit mobility when practicing on the floor. For people with knee problems, landing can be difficult. But, says Stewart, “basically, aerial silk brings them to the surface.”

The benefits of aerial yoga-

A combination of the arts and athletics, aerial yoga offers several physical and psychological benefits:

  1. Improved flexibility, stability, and balance. A 2019 study observed that aerial athletes have exceptional flexibility, balance, and strength.
  2. Traction and joint decompression. Hanging upside down and inverting are said to decompress the joints, which can compress over time due to gravity and age.
  3. Reduced risk factors for heart disease. While research on this style of yoga is limited, the most-cited study, conducted in 2016 by the American Council on Exercise (ACE), found a significant reduction in the risk factors associated with cardiovascular heart disease.
  4. Low to moderate intensity. The same ACE-commissioned study found that 50 minutes of aerial yoga can burn upwards of 300 calories, making it a form of low to moderate-intensity exercise.
  5. Improved mental health. A small study noted significant improvements in depression and stress levels when people practiced aerial arts for the love of movement. This seems to bode particularly well for aerial yoga, which applies yogic philosophies and is often less competitive and more accessible than a circus-based or formal acrobatics class.

Testimonials

  • Aerial yoga is not recommended for people who are pregnant or for whom hanging upside down is not medically advised. This includes, but is not limited to, people with eye conditions such as cataracts or detached retina, as well as people with unregulated blood pressure. Check with a healthcare professional before signing up for your first class.
